# Attorney-Client Privilege Playbook (reference)

The full ruleset behind `legal-comms-check`. General U.S. attorney-client
privilege guidance. Use this to assess and revise any communication to/with/about
Legal. Section order: core rules → what is / isn't protected → marking guide →
decision guide → review checklist → revision patterns → worked before/after
examples → knowledge check.

---

## 1. Core rules

- **Primary-purpose test.** A communication is privileged only if its *primary
  purpose* is to **request or provide legal advice**. Involving a lawyer, or
  labeling something "Privileged," does not create privilege.
- **Only legal advice is protected.** Legal advice = the *legal implications* of
  business choices: (i) counseling on legal risks, and (ii) identifying ways to
  achieve business objectives in compliance with the law.
- **Business advice is not protected** — financial, operational, technical,
  strategic, reputational, public-policy, or public-relations input, even when
  it comes from an attorney.
- **Most material is not privileged**, even with attorneys involved, because
  most of it is primarily business. When a business document contains legal
  advice, the legal advice is redacted and the rest is produced — so legal
  advice must be *identifiable*.
- **Over-marking is a risk**, not a safe default: it invites court/regulator
  skepticism and erodes credibility for genuinely privileged material.

## 2. What is NOT protected

- **Facts.** Facts are essentially never privileged and cannot be shielded by
  telling a lawyer or by stamping "Privileged"/"Seeking Advice" on a thread.
- **Visibility/FYI to Legal.** Merely copying or "adding counsel to advise"
  with no specific legal question is generally not privileged.
- **Business discussions**, even sensitive ones, and even if a lawyer chimes in
  on the business angle.
- **Third-party–shared content.** Sharing privileged material with third
  parties generally **waives** privilege. Third parties include ex-employees,
  suppliers, contractors, sellers — anyone not employed by the company (except
  outside counsel supporting the legal work).
- **Widely distributed legal advice.** Over-broad internal circulation (large
  DLs, listservs, open Slack/Teams channels; feeding into AI tools without Legal
  approval) can waive privilege.
- **Regulatory facts/compliance debates.** Whether an activity *complies* (legal
  conclusion) can be privileged; *how* the company complies (facts) and business
  debates about how to comply generally are not — courts read "regulatory
  compliance" narrowly, so name the specific law/regulation/matter.
- **Deal material.** Business strategy, deal-term summaries, deal rationales,
  retrospectives, and most approvals are not privileged unless they contain
  actual legal advice. Mark sensitive deal docs "Company Confidential," not
  "Privileged."

## 3. What IS protected (and how to keep it that way)

- A confidential communication between attorney and client whose primary purpose
  is legal advice.
- In a *mixed* communication, the legal-advice portion — **only if it is
  signposted or sectioned off** so it can be identified and redacted.
- Attorney work product: documents prepared by/at the direction of an attorney
  **because of** anticipated litigation (not in the ordinary course of
  business). Apply the work-product label — it is often the only way to identify
  and protect it. Don't share work product with third parties without litigation
  counsel's approval.
- Keep it protected by: narrow need-to-know distribution, no third-party
  sharing, clear marking, and clear signposting.

## 4. Marking guide

| Situation | Mark as | Also do |
|---|---|---|
| Primary purpose is to seek/convey legal advice | **Privileged & Confidential** | Make the legal ask/advice explicit |
| Mixed legal + business content | **Confidential – Contains Legal Advice** | Signpost legal advice ("Legal advises…") **or** put it in a labeled "Legal Considerations" section |
| Sensitive business / deal document | **Company Confidential** | Do **not** use "Privileged" |
| Just giving Legal visibility / FYI | *(no privilege marking)* | Don't add shorthand "privileged" tags |
| Attorney work product (litigation) | **Attorney Work Product / Privileged & Confidential** | Confirm anticipated-litigation basis with counsel |
| Non-legal templates/forms, mislabeled long-form memos | *(remove P&C)* | Strip inaccurate "Privileged & Confidential" labels |

## 5. Decision guide

1. Is the **primary purpose** legal advice (legal risk, legality, compliance-
   with-law)? **No →** not privileged; remove privilege marks; treat as business
   (use "Company Confidential" if sensitive). **Yes →** continue.
2. Is there a **specific request** for legal advice (names the attorney + the
   concrete risk/law/decision)? If no → rewrite the ask.
3. Does it **mix** legal and business? If yes → signpost or section the legal
   part; consider splitting into two communications.
4. Is the **distribution** need-to-know only? If no → trim recipients.
5. Any **third party** on it (or being forwarded externally)? If yes → stop;
   route through Legal.
6. **International**? Privilege is weaker/absent abroad — consult a
   country-specific legal POC; in some places outside counsel must be on it.
7. Apply the correct **marking** from §4 and append the standard caveat.

## 6. Review checklist (run on every draft)

- [ ] Primary purpose is genuinely legal advice (else: don't mark privileged)
- [ ] Specific, pointed legal question naming the attorney
- [ ] No vague "please advise," "seeking legal advice," "maintaining privilege"
- [ ] Legal vs business separated/signposted if mixed
- [ ] Correct marking per §4 (no over-marking)
- [ ] Recipients limited to need-to-know; no broad DLs/channels/AI tools
- [ ] No third parties; nothing being forwarded outside the company
- [ ] No attempt to "privilege" facts by routing them through a lawyer
- [ ] Meeting notes: legal-advice portions marked, attorney named, no verbatim
- [ ] International sharing flagged if applicable
- [ ] Standard caveat included

## 7. Revision patterns

- **Vague ask → specific ask.** "Please advise" → "Attorney X, do you see any
  legal risks under [specific law] if we [specific action]?"
- **Mixed message → separated.** Keep the business discussion in the body; move
  legal questions into a labeled "Legal Considerations" block, or send a
  separate note to counsel whose sole purpose is the legal question.
- **Over-marked → right-sized.** Business doc stamped "Privileged & Confidential"
  → "Company Confidential" (or no marking) with a note on what (if anything) is
  actually for Legal.
- **Broad distribution → need-to-know.** Remove large DLs/listservs/open
  channels; send legal advice only to those who need it for their role.
- **FYI to Legal → labeled correctly.** Drop the privilege tag; state it's for
  visibility, not a legal-advice request (or turn it into a real, specific ask).
- **Meeting invite.** Title/agenda should state whether the primary purpose is
  legal advice; set the notes posture accordingly (mark + signpost legal
  portions, name the attorney, avoid verbatim quotes).

## 8. Worked before/after examples

**Email — vague, over-marked**
> Before — *Subject: Project Falcon — Privileged & Confidential*
> "Adding Legal. Please advise. Also looping in the launch DL for awareness."

> After — *Subject: Project Falcon — pricing change* (no privilege mark on the
> business thread)
> "Attorney X — separate from the business discussion below: do you see any
> antitrust risk if we move to the tiered pricing in option B? Flagging just to
> you for a legal read." *(Business discussion stays with the team; the specific
> legal question goes only to the attorney.)*

**Doc — mixed content**
> Before — whole internal memo stamped "Privileged & Confidential" because it
> mentions legal risk in one paragraph.

> After — doc marked "Confidential – Contains Legal Advice"; the legal paragraph
> moved into a "Legal Considerations" section (or prefixed "Legal advises…"), so
> it can be identified and redacted while the business content is produced.

**Slack — facts**
> Before — "Privileged & Confidential: heads up, we shipped 4,000 units without
> the required warning label."

> After — recognize the fact is not privileged regardless of the tag. If a legal
> question is needed: "Attorney X — given we shipped units missing the [law]
> warning label, what are our legal obligations/exposure?" (specific legal ask),
> kept to need-to-know recipients.

## 9. Knowledge check

1. The attorney-client privilege protects the confidentiality of: **communications
   between attorneys and clients for the primary purpose of requesting or
   providing legal advice.**
2. Legal advice generally concerns: **the legal implications of business choices,
   including advice on ways to achieve business objectives in compliance with
   the law.**
3. Adding an attorney to an email chain about business issues will: **not trigger
   the attorney-client privilege or protect the email chain from disclosure.**
4. Who should take the lead on marking documents/communications as privileged:
   **attorneys — they are best equipped to make privilege calls.**
5. If you prepare a business document that contains legal advice from your
   attorney, you should: **all of the above** — label it "Confidential –
   Contains Legal Advice"; put legal advice in a separate labeled section or
   signpost it; share only with employees who need it; and not share with third
   parties unless approved by Legal.
